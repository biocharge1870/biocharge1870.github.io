Baseline Technical Analysis

From piezo properties to power

Finding the right material

Before the team could begin defining the power output we needed to find a suitable material to build around. We had to maximize the energy potential with the budget at our disposal. To do this, we needed to maximize the electromechanical coupling factor, k, of our choosen material. This property defines the efficiency of the material by relating the internal electrical energy to the exhibited mechanical energy under a constant tensor field and electrical field respectively. To maximize this term, we needed a material with a large piezoelectric charge constant, d. The piezoelectric constant is a readily available material property of piezoelectric materials, defining the charge generated per newton of force applied. This term changes with the orientation of the poling in a particular piece so to maximize it and by effect the electromechanical coupling, a longitudinal mode based material was chosen, macro fiber composite (MFC) type M4010P1.







Defining power output per step

Taking the piezoelectric material properties, charge and voltage constants d and g, with the force of the average persons heel strike the team was able to derive a power output relative to the compressive stresses of walking over a 1 Hz frequency gait. It was also important to consider the angle of attack for the strikes as this would influence the extent of the strain experienced by the material. The resulting formula provided us with a maxium theoretical power output per piece, per step, and we were able to extrapolate this value out over the optimal placement of stacked MFC components.

Induction charging

The stator and the mover

In order for the BioCharge device to consistently provide users with a charge, our team has decided to incorporate both piezoelectric and electromagnetic power generation modes into our build. By combining the systems we are able to generate energy from the impact of the step via piezo components and through the walk cycle via an induction coil built into the shoe harness. The induction coil operates by placing a series of sectioned out magnets, known as the mover, into a static tube, known as the stator, which is wound in alternating directions with copper wire. The mover is free to oscillate within the confines of the stator throughout the stepping motion and this cycling allows for charge to be produced and collected simply by walking around. This energy is generated due to Faraday's law. As the mover cycles through its motion it changes the magnetic environment around the copper wiring of the stator and induces a voltage on the coil that can be directed into capacitors which will ultimately offload into the battery system. Preliminary research indicates a power output potential of 20 milliwatts that will add onto the generation potential already realized by the teams piezoelectric material based build.







Electrical Analysis

After the energy is generated, it must be converted and stored for later use.

Convert to a direct current

Using a bridge rectifier circuit that features multiple diodes, the reversed current would be redirected to the desired source.

Store the electricy

Using a thin Lithium Polymer (LiPo) battery, as well as an overdischarge protection circuit, the generated electricity can be stored for later use.

Charge your device

Once enough energy has been generated and stored, the PCB design to the right is set up to increase the voltage to an optimal magnitude to charge a phone.

Budgeting Basics

Piezo material cost?

The M4010P1 macro fiber composite costs $49 per piece, cheaper than all other varients. The majority of the budget will be used to purchase as much piezoelectric material as possible to maximize power output.

Binding component costs?

The team aims to buy a second hand snowboard binding for $50-$75 as well as budgetting out additional funds for smaller components. The lower elements of the construction will be primarily aluminum based to help mitigate costs.

Electrical considerations

The majority of the electrical costs will go towards the LiPo battery, which is not too expensive, as we are not dealing with intense amounts of electricity. For a battery that can effectively charge an iPhone, we are at about $16. The PCB design is not expensive at all, being around $10 to print and deliver.

Base of the device

The stacking of piezoeelctric components and placemet of the induction coil will be housed in a low profile wood and felt assembly meant to keep costs down in the final prototyping phase and to get the most out of the strain induced on the piezo pieces.
